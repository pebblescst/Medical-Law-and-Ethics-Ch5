Chapter focus: Chapter 5 moves from the foundations of law (Ch. 4) to the practical realities of professional liability — what creates liability, how negligence is proven using the Four Ds, how lawsuits proceed, how disputes can be resolved outside court, and the critical doctrine of informed consent that governs the patient-provider relationship.
At a Glance — Three Types of Professional Liability Claims
Malfeasance
Performance of a totally wrongful and unlawful act. The practitioner does something that is completely wrong and unauthorized — not just done improperly, but wrong to do at all. Example: a medical assistant who is not licensed to prescribe medications does so anyway.
Misfeasance
Performance of a lawful act in an illegal or improper manner. The act itself is permissible, but the way it is carried out is wrong. Example: a nurse administers the correct medication but uses the wrong route, injuring the patient.
Nonfeasance
The failure to act when one should. The practitioner had a duty to act but did not — an omission rather than a commission. Example: a nurse who observes a patient fall and fails to report or assess the patient fails their duty of care.
Memory tool: Mal- = bad/wrong (wrong act entirely). Mis- = improper (right act, wrong way). Non- = none (no act when required). All three can form the basis of a professional liability claim.
Liability & Law of Agency
Liability
All competent adults are liable — legally responsible — for their own actions on the job and in private life. In healthcare, this means every practitioner is personally accountable for how they perform their clinical and professional duties. Liability cannot be transferred away simply by following orders — a nurse who carries out an unlawful physician order may still be personally liable.
Law of agency
The law governing the relationship between a principal (the employer/physician) and an agent (the employee/staff member who acts on behalf of the principal). The agent acts for the principal; the principal is legally bound by the agent's authorized acts. May be expressed (explicit delegation) or implied (arising from the nature of the employment relationship).
Agent
One who acts for and on behalf of another (the principal). In a medical practice: the medical assistant, nurse, or other staff member is the agent; the physician or employing organization is the principal. The agent's authorized actions legally bind the principal.
Respondeat superior
"Let the master answer" — the Latin phrase describing the legal doctrine that the physician (employer) is responsible for the acts of their employees performed within the scope of employment. If a medical assistant commits a negligent act while working for a physician, the physician can be held vicariously liable even if the physician was not present and did not personally commit the error. This is the primary reason physicians carry professional liability insurance.
Critical point: Respondeat superior does NOT eliminate the employee's personal liability — it adds the employer's liability on top of the employee's. Both the medical assistant AND the physician can be named defendants in a lawsuit arising from the employee's negligent act.
Physician Liability as an Employer
Beyond respondeat superior, a physician-employer faces direct liability for conditions of the physical practice environment. Three specific areas:
Grounds and buildings
The physician is responsible for maintaining safe physical premises — including parking lots, walkways, waiting rooms, exam rooms, and all facilities. A patient who slips on an icy walkway or is injured by a defective examination table can sue the physician-employer under premises liability. Regular safety inspections and prompt hazard remediation are required.
Automobiles
If a physician or employee uses a vehicle for work purposes (e.g., house calls, delivering supplies, errands on behalf of the practice) and an accident occurs, the physician-employer may be vicariously liable. The "scope of employment" at the time of the accident is the key legal question.
Employee safety
Physicians who employ staff must comply with OSHA standards — including the Bloodborne Pathogen Standard, HCS, hazardous waste protocols, and general workplace safety requirements. An employee injured due to an unsafe workplace can pursue workers' compensation AND may pursue OSHA action against the employer.
Standard of Care, Duty of Care & Reasonable Person Standard
Standard of care
The level of performance expected of a healthcare worker carrying out their professional duties — what a reasonably prudent practitioner of the same training, specialty, and experience would do under the same circumstances. The benchmark against which a defendant's conduct is measured in negligence cases.
Duty of care
The legal obligation of healthcare workers to patients and non-patients. When a duty of care exists (from the physician-patient relationship or other special relationships), failure to meet the standard of care can establish negligence liability.
Reasonable person standard
The standard of behavior that judges a person's actions "according to what a reasonable person would or would not do under similar circumstances." All competent adults — healthcare providers included — are held responsible for their actions (or failure to act) under this standard. It is an objective standard, not based on what the specific defendant believed was reasonable.
Healthcare professionals are held to a HIGHER standard: While the general population is held to the "reasonable person" standard, healthcare practitioners are held to the standard of a "reasonable practitioner of the same training and specialty." A physician is not compared to an average citizen — they are compared to what a reasonably competent physician in the same specialty would do.
Guidelines for Healthcare Practice — Protecting Against Liability
Following these guidelines consistently is the best defense against professional liability claims. Each represents a recognized standard of safe and ethical healthcare practice.
Practice within scope of training
Use professional title commensurate with education
Maintain confidentiality at all times
Prepare and maintain health records
Document accurately and completely
Use appropriate ethical and legal guidelines for patient info
Follow employer's established policies
Follow appropriate legal guidelines
Maintain and dispose of controlled substances properly
Follow risk management protocols
Meet requirements for credentialing
Help develop and maintain policies
Documentation is your best defense: "If it wasn't documented, it wasn't done." Accurate, timely, complete medical records are the practitioner's primary protection in any lawsuit. Altered or incomplete records are often the most damaging evidence against a defendant in a malpractice case.
The Four Ds of Negligence
All four elements must be proven by the plaintiff for a successful negligence (malpractice) claim. If any one of the four is absent, the negligence claim fails. These are the same four elements introduced in Ch. 4 — here they are presented using the "Four Ds" mnemonic widely used in healthcare law education.
D
Duty
The person charged owed a duty of care to the person bringing the claim. In healthcare, duty is established when the physician-patient relationship exists. Once a practitioner accepts a patient, a duty of care arises automatically. The duty to care for a patient continues until the relationship is properly terminated. Duty can also arise in non-patient relationships — for example, a physician who begins to assist an accident victim in a public setting may create a duty to continue that care.
D
Dereliction (Breach of Duty)
The healthcare provider breached the duty of care to the patient — they failed to meet the standard of care. Dereliction is established by comparing what the defendant did (or failed to do) with what a reasonably prudent practitioner of the same specialty would have done. Expert witnesses typically testify about the standard of care and whether the defendant's actions constituted a breach. Remember malfeasance (wrong act), misfeasance (lawful act done improperly), and nonfeasance (failure to act) — all three can constitute dereliction.
D
Damage
Court-ordered monetary awards given to patients as a result of legally recognized injuries. The plaintiff must prove they suffered actual, identifiable harm — physical, psychological, economic, or a combination. Without provable damages, there is no valid negligence claim even if duty and dereliction are established. Five types of damage awards exist (see below). A deviation from standard of care that causes no harm is not actionable negligence.
D
Direct Cause (Causation)
The breach of duty of care to the patient directly caused the injury. Two components: (1) Actual causation ("but for" test) — but for the provider's breach, the injury would not have occurred; (2) Proximate cause (foreseeability) — the patient's injury was a reasonably foreseeable result of the breach. Causation is often the most contested element in malpractice cases, particularly when patients have pre-existing conditions that complicate attribution of harm.
Five Types of Damage Awards
General compensatory
To compensate for injuries or losses resulting directly from the violation of the patient's rights — pain and suffering, disability, disfigurement, and loss of enjoyment of life. These are actual losses but not easily quantifiable in dollars; the jury assigns a dollar value.
Special compensatory
To compensate for specific, quantifiable losses not directly caused by the wrong itself — lost wages, medical expenses, costs of future care, rehabilitation costs, and other economic losses. These can be calculated with a specific dollar figure.
Consequential
To compensate for losses caused indirectly by a product defect or wrongful act — losses that flow naturally from the harm but are a step removed from the direct injury. Common in product liability cases where a defective device causes a cascade of downstream problems.
Punitive
To punish the offender and deter similar conduct. Awarded above and beyond actual damages when the defendant's conduct was especially egregious, reckless, or malicious. Not available in ordinary negligence — requires a higher level of wrongdoing. Can be very large awards designed to send a deterrent message.
Nominal
A token amount (e.g., $1) awarded to recognize that the plaintiff's rights were technically violated — even though no actual monetary loss was proved. Vindicates the principle that a legal wrong occurred; rare in healthcare malpractice but may appear in rights-based cases.
Res Ipsa Loquitur & Wrongful Death
Doctrine of Common Knowledge — Res Ipsa Loquitur:
"The thing speaks for itself" — a doctrine applied when negligence is so obvious that expert testimony is not needed to establish it.
Three conditions for res ipsa loquitur:
Under defendant's control
The act causing harm must obviously have been under the defendant's control — the injury could only have been caused by the defendant's actions
Patient did not contribute
The patient must not have contributed to the act — no contributory negligence on the plaintiff's part
Reasonable care would have prevented it
It must be apparent that the patient would not have been injured if reasonable care had been used
Classic healthcare examples:
Foreign object left in body during surgery Burns to anesthetized patient Damage to healthy tissue during surgery Infection from unsterilized instruments
Wrongful Death Statutes:
State statutes that allow a deceased person's beneficiaries to collect for loss to the estate for future earnings when a death is judged to have been caused by negligence.

✦ Beneficiaries (spouse, children, dependents) may sue for economic damages — lost future earnings, loss of consortium, funeral expenses
✦ The state may also prosecute a healthcare practitioner under criminal statutes for the wrongful death of a patient (criminal negligence, reckless homicide)
Four Phases of a Medical Malpractice Lawsuit
Understanding the lawsuit process is essential for healthcare practitioners — if named in a lawsuit, you will encounter all of these phases. Most malpractice cases settle before or during trial; only a small percentage reach a jury verdict.
Phase 1
Pleading Phase
The lawsuit formally begins when the plaintiff files a complaint (also called a petition) with the court, stating the allegations and the legal basis for the claim. The court then issues a summons — a formal legal notice delivered to the defendant that a lawsuit has been filed and that they must respond. The defendant must file an answer (responding to each allegation) within a specified time period or risk a default judgment. The pleading phase establishes the legal issues and the parties' initial positions. In healthcare: the named defendants (physician, hospital, staff member) are informed and must immediately notify their malpractice insurance carrier.
Phase 2
Interrogatory / Pretrial Discovery Phase
Both sides gather evidence through the formal discovery process. Key mechanisms:

Interrogatories: Written questions that must be answered under oath within a specified time. Both sides submit interrogatories to the opposing party covering facts, witnesses, and evidence.

Subpoena: A court order requiring a person to appear and testify, or to produce documents (subpoena duces tecum — "bring with you"). Medical records, billing records, and personnel files may all be subpoenaed.

Deposition: Out-of-court testimony given under oath, recorded by a court reporter. Attorneys for both sides can question the witness. Depositions are used to lock in testimony, discover facts, and assess witness credibility. Healthcare practitioners named in lawsuits are almost always deposed — answer questions truthfully and avoid volunteering information not asked.
Phase 3
Trial Phase
The formal court proceeding in which the case is presented to a judge and/or jury.

Jury selection (voir dire): Potential jurors are questioned by attorneys; both sides may challenge jurors for cause (bias) or use peremptory challenges (no reason required, limited number).

Opening statements: Both attorneys outline the case they expect to prove.

Witness testimony: Witnesses are called by both sides and may be cross-examined by opposing counsel. In malpractice: expert witnesses are crucial — they establish the standard of care and testify whether the defendant met or breached it. Fact witnesses testify about what they personally observed.

Closing arguments: Attorneys summarize the evidence and their interpretation of the law.

Jury deliberation and verdict: The jury reaches a verdict on liability and, if liable, the amount of damages. In medical malpractice, the standard of proof is preponderance of the evidence — more likely than not (>50%).
Phase 4
Appeals Phase
Either party may request that a higher court review the case if they believe legal errors occurred in the trial court proceedings — for example, incorrect jury instructions, improper admission of evidence, or procedural misconduct. Important distinctions:

✦ Appeals courts do NOT re-try the facts — they review only questions of law
✦ The appeals court reviews the trial record and briefs submitted by both parties
✦ Possible outcomes: affirm the lower court's decision, reverse it, or remand (send back for a new trial)
✦ Multiple levels of appeal may be available — state appellate court → state supreme court; federal circuit → U.S. Supreme Court (if a federal question is involved)
Fact witness
Provides facts that the witness personally observed — what they saw, heard, said, or did. Cannot offer opinions on whether the standard of care was met. Healthcare staff members are commonly called as fact witnesses to describe the events surrounding the alleged negligence.
Expert witness
Has education, skills, knowledge, and experience in a relevant field that qualifies them to give opinions — not just facts. In malpractice: experts establish what the standard of care was and whether the defendant met or breached it. Expert testimony is typically required in cases that are not res ipsa loquitur.
Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)
Definition: Techniques for resolving civil disputes without going to court. Uses neutral mediators or arbitrators. ADR is widely used in medical malpractice because trials are expensive, time-consuming, unpredictable, and damaging to all parties' reputations. ADR is most effective when both parties want a resolution, the dispute is primarily factual rather than legal, and when the relationship between the parties may need to be preserved.
🤝
Mediation
A method of settling disputes without going to court using a neutral third party (the mediator) who facilitates communication and negotiation between the parties.

Key characteristics:
✦ Voluntary — both parties must agree to participate; cannot be forced into mediation
✦ Neutral mediator — the mediator has no stake in the outcome; serves as a facilitator, not a decision-maker
✦ Mediator CANNOT impose a solution — the mediator can suggest options and help parties understand each other's perspectives, but any resolution must be mutually agreed upon by both parties
✦ Confidential — statements made in mediation are typically not admissible in later court proceedings
✦ Flexible — can address both legal and non-legal needs (apology, policy changes, emotional acknowledgment)

Best for: Cases where the relationship between parties has some value to preserve; cases with strong emotional components; cases where flexibility in remedy is desirable.
⚖️
Arbitration
A method of settling disputes in which opposing parties agree to abide by the decision of an arbitrator — a private, binding adjudication process that replaces a court trial.

Key characteristics:
✦ Binding — unlike mediation, the arbitrator's decision (award) is final and enforceable; extremely limited grounds for appeal
✦ Arbitrator selection: An arbitrator may be chosen by the court, by the American Arbitration Association (AAA), or by the parties themselves. In a three-arbitrator panel: each party selects one arbitrator, and those two arbitrators select a neutral third
✦ More formal than mediation but less formal than a court trial
✦ Faster and less expensive than litigation
✦ Private — not a public record as court proceedings are

Best for: Cases where a final, enforceable decision is needed; cases where the parties have a pre-dispute arbitration agreement (common in managed care contracts and employment agreements).
ADR in healthcare context: Many managed care contracts and hospital employment agreements include mandatory arbitration clauses — requiring that all disputes go to arbitration rather than court. Patients may be asked to sign arbitration agreements before receiving care. The enforceability of these agreements varies by state. As a healthcare professional, you may encounter both mediation and arbitration in your career — understanding both processes protects your interests.
Consent & Informed Consent
Consent: Permission from a person — either expressed or implied — for something to be done by another. In healthcare, consent is permission to examine the patient, perform diagnostic tests, and treat the patient.
Doctrine of Informed Consent — required elements (outlined in state medical practice acts):
1
Proposed modes of treatment — the patient must be told specifically what procedure or treatment is being proposed and how it will be performed
2
Why the treatment is necessary — the reason the proposed treatment is recommended; the nature of the condition being treated and why this approach is chosen
3
Risks involved in the proposed treatment — material risks that a reasonable patient would want to know before deciding; includes both common risks and serious but rare risks
4
Available alternative modes of treatment — the patient must be informed of other treatment options, including less invasive alternatives, so they can make a truly informed choice
5
Risks of alternative modes of treatment — the risks associated with each alternative option, enabling meaningful comparison
6
Risks involved if treatment is refused — the patient must understand the likely consequences of declining the proposed treatment; this enables an informed refusal as well as an informed acceptance
Who obtains informed consent: The physician or practitioner performing the procedure is responsible for obtaining informed consent — NOT the medical assistant or nurse. Support staff may witness the patient's signature but cannot provide the substantive information required for consent. Delegating the consent conversation to unqualified staff is itself a breach of the informed consent doctrine.
Who CANNOT Give Informed Consent
The following individuals lack the legal capacity to give informed consent on their own behalf. Consent must be obtained from a legally authorized surrogate.
Minors — persons under the age of majority
Generally those under 18. A parent or legal guardian must give informed consent on the minor's behalf. Exceptions: emancipated minors (legally independent of parents), mature minors (state-specific), and for specific services in many states (STI treatment, substance abuse, contraception, mental health counseling). Always know your state's specific rules.
Mentally incompetent persons (court-determined)
Persons who have been adjudicated (legally declared) incompetent by a court — a legal guardian is appointed to make decisions. Note: incompetence is a legal finding, not a clinical one. A person may be cognitively impaired without being legally incompetent. Evaluate actual decision-making capacity (can the person understand, reason, appreciate, and communicate?) separately from legal status.
Persons speaking limited or no English
Cannot give meaningful informed consent without understanding the information provided. Facilities must provide qualified interpreters — not family members or untrained staff. Using a professional medical interpreter is required. Signing a consent form in a language the patient does not understand is not valid consent.
Special Consent Situations
Informed consent and abortion law
States impose a wide variety of restrictions on abortion — mandatory waiting periods, required counseling, specific information that must be disclosed, gestational limits, and facility requirements. Laws vary dramatically by state and change frequently. Healthcare practitioners must stay informed about current law in their specific state. Practitioners should check state health department websites and legal resources regularly.
Informed consent and HIV testing
State public health laws vary significantly for HIV testing:
✦ Infants and young children: Permission from legal guardian (may be a parent) is required
✦ Married minors: May have the right to consent to testing independently
✦ Emancipated minors: May consent independently
✦ Minor parents: May have the right to consent for themselves and their child
Always verify current state law — HIV testing consent requirements are an area of active legislative change.
When Consent Is Unnecessary
Emergency situations
When a patient is incapacitated and immediate treatment is necessary to save life or prevent serious harm, the law implies consent — a reasonable person in that situation would have consented if able. The emergency exception applies only when the threat is immediate and serious, and when there is no time to obtain consent from a surrogate. Document the emergency carefully.
Good Samaritan acts — four requirements
Healthcare practitioners who voluntarily assist in emergencies outside their workplace (accidents, public settings) are protected by Good Samaritan laws — provided they:
✦ Give care in good faith — genuine intent to help, not to practice professionally
✦ Act within scope of training and knowledge — do not exceed your competence
✦ Use due care under circumstances — reasonable care given the emergency environment
✦ Do NOT bill for services — Good Samaritan protection evaporates if compensation is sought
